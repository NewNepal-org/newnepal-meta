# Case Entry: Nepal Airlines Wide-Body Aircraft Procurement Scandal

---

## Basic Information

**Title**: Nepal Airlines Wide-Body Aircraft Procurement Irregularities

**Date Range**:
- **Start Date**: 2016-04-14 [NAC Board decision to initiate procurement]
- **End Date**: Ongoing [Supreme Court appeal filed 2025-12-02]

---

## Case Summary

### Short Description (1-2 sentences)
Nepal Airlines Corporation's 2017 procurement of two Airbus A330-200 wide-body aircraft for $209.6 million involved systematic corruption, including bid-rigging, use of shell companies, and manipulation of technical specifications, resulting in confirmed losses of NPR 1.47 billion ($13.4 million) with broader irregularities estimated at NPR 4.35 billion ($37 million).

### Detailed Description
In 2016, Nepal Airlines Corporation (NAC) initiated the largest aviation procurement in Nepal's history—acquiring two Airbus A330-200 wide-body aircraft for approximately $209.6 million to expand its international fleet. Rather than purchasing new aircraft directly from Airbus, NAC controversially opted to buy slightly used planes (with approximately 1,000 flight hours) through a complex chain of intermediaries led by US-based AAR Corp, which created a special purpose vehicle (SPV) in Ireland called HiFly X Ireland Ltd to handle the transaction.


Parliamentary investigations revealed that NAC breached the Public Procurement Act by altering aircraft specifications post-bidding. The initial Request for Proposal specified aircraft with a Maximum Takeoff Weight (MTOW) of 242 tonnes, but NAC accepted planes with only 230 tonnes MTOW without any price reduction. This 12-tonne downgrade significantly affected the aircraft's range and payload capacity, causing an estimated loss of $8.4 million alone.

The Public Accounts Committee (PAC) investigation in 2019 concluded that the procurement caused losses of NPR 4.35 billion, identifying NAC Managing Director Sugat Ratna Kansakar as the "kingpin" of the scheme. The deal violated procurement laws by tailor-making the tender for specific suppliers, invoking a bylaw clause meant for "old" aircraft to justify buying through intermediaries rather than directly from the manufacturer, and creating an Irish shell company (HiFly X) to obscure the money trail.

Following the PAC report, the Commission for the Investigation of Abuse of Authority (CIAA) filed corruption charges on 2024-04-04 against 32 individuals and two companies, claiming losses of NPR 1.47 billion. On 2024-12-05, the Special Court convicted 10 individuals, including former NAC Managing Director Sugat Ratna Kansakar, former Tourism Secretary Shankar Prasad Adhikari, and several foreign nationals, while acquitting 21 others including former Tourism Minister Jeevan Bahadur Shahi.

The verdict sparked controversy when the Special Court criticized the CIAA for "selective prosecution," noting that Prem Kumar Rai—the former Tourism Secretary who chaired the initial board meeting that approved the procurement plan and later became CIAA Chief Commissioner—was not charged. The CIAA has appealed to the Supreme Court seeking to convict those acquitted.

In a parallel development, AAR Corp admitted to paying $2.5 million in bribes to Nepali officials and agreed to pay $55 million in fines to the US Department of Justice for Foreign Corrupt Practices Act violations. The two aircraft, named "Annapurna" and "Makalu," continue to operate in NAC's fleet as reminders of one of Nepal's largest corruption scandals.

## Key Allegations

1. **Violation of Public Procurement Act**: NAC bypassed standard competitive bidding requirements by invoking Clause 236(1) of NAC Financial Bylaws meant for "old" aircraft, despite seeking essentially new planes (1,000 flight hours), to avoid purchasing directly from Airbus manufacturer.

2. **Bid-Rigging and Insider Information**: AAR Corp received confidential RFP drafts months in advance; on 2016-09-26, AAR executive Deepak Sharma emailed colleagues stating the RFP was "designed for us"; tender specifications were tailored to match AAR's inventory.

3. **Illegal Use of Shell Company**: Contract was transferred from evaluated consortium (AAR Corp, German Aviation Capital, HiFly) to HiFly X Ireland Limited, a special purpose vehicle incorporated 2017-02-21 with only $1 paid-up capital, not evaluated during bidding process, stripping NAC of financial security.

4. **Technical Specification Manipulation (MTOW Fraud)**: NAC altered specifications post-bidding to accept aircraft with 230 tonnes MTOW instead of contracted 242 tonnes without price reduction; 12-tonne downgrade significantly reduced range and payload capacity, causing estimated loss of $8.4 million.

5. **Price Inflation Without Justification**: Despite purchasing "used" aircraft (1,000 flight hours), NAC did not secure any depreciation discount; price was escalated to maximum, causing approximately $6.8 million in undue costs.

6. **Excessive Overpayment**: Purchase price was approximately $23.6 million higher than benchmark price lists suggested for aircraft of that age and condition.

7. **Bribery and Kickbacks**: AAR Corp admitted to paying $2.5 million in bribes to Nepali officials through intermediaries to secure the deal; US DOJ investigation confirmed systematic corruption.

8. **Tailor-Made Tender Specifications**: RFP illegally specified particular aircraft models (Airbus A330-200) and engine brands (Rolls-Royce Trent 772B), violating procurement laws requiring brand-neutral specifications to ensure fair competition.

9. **Wasteful Expenditures**: 38-member delegation traveled to France for aircraft inspection, adding unnecessary costs to procurement.

10. **Selective Prosecution and Conflict of Interest**: CIAA failed to charge Prem Kumar Rai, who chaired the 2016-04-14 board meeting initiating the procurement, despite his central role; Rai later became CIAA Chief Commissioner, creating institutional conflict of interest.

11. **Unauthorized Contract Transfer**: Final Sales and Purchase Agreement was signed with HiFly X Ireland (shell company) rather than original consortium members, violating procurement rules against third-party contract transfers.

12. **Lack of Manufacturer Warranty**: By purchasing through intermediaries rather than directly from Airbus, NAC lost manufacturer warranties and quality guarantees typically provided with new aircraft.

## Timeline

### Event 1
- **Date**: 2015
- **Title**: NAC Formulates 10-Year Business Plan
- **Description**: Under Managing Director Sugat Ratna Kansakar, Nepal Airlines Corporation formulated a strategic business plan to acquire two wide-body aircraft capable of non-stop operations to Sydney and direct flights to European capitals. The technical requirement specified Maximum Takeoff Weight (MTOW) of 242 tonnes, critical for Kathmandu's high-altitude airport (1,338 meters) to reach distant destinations. This was NAC's attempt to revitalize long-haul capabilities after years of decline.

### Event 2
- **Date**: 2016-04-14
- **Title**: NAC Board Approves Procurement Plan Using "Old Aircraft" Loophole
- **Description**: NAC Board of Directors met under chairmanship of Prem Kumar Rai (then Tourism Secretary). Board made pivotal decision to proceed with procurement under Clause 236(1) of NAC Financial Bylaws, 2065—a provision designed for second-hand/used aircraft—rather than standard provisions for new equipment. Board simultaneously set condition that aircraft must have flown no more than 1,000 hours. This contradictory decision (1,000-hour aircraft are essentially new) created legal loophole to exclude Airbus manufacturer and introduce intermediaries. Board formed procurement sub-committee to execute the plan.

### Event 3
- **Date**: 2016-09-26
- **Title**: Request for Proposal (RFP) Issued with Suspicious Specifications
- **Description**: NAC officially issued RFP for two Airbus A330-200 aircraft. Specifications included: manufactured after January 2014, fewer than 1,000 flight hours, MTOW of 242 tonnes, Rolls-Royce Trent 772B engines. On the same day, Deepak Sharma (AAR Corp President, International) emailed colleagues stating the RFP was "designed for us." US DOJ investigation later revealed Sharma had received confidential RFP drafts months in advance, confirming bid-rigging. The specific brand requirements (Airbus, Rolls-Royce) violated Public Procurement Act provisions requiring brand-neutral specifications.

### Event 4
- **Date**: 2016-11
- **Title**: AAR-Led Consortium Selected
- **Description**: NAC selected consortium comprising AAR Corp (USA), German Aviation Capital (Germany), and HiFly (Portugal) to supply two A330-200 aircraft at $104.8 million each (total $209.6 million). Selection process was compromised by insider information provided to AAR Corp. The consortium proposed to supply aircraft matching the tailored specifications.

### Event 5
- **Date**: 2017-01-27
- **Title**: Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) Signed
- **Description**: NAC signed MoU with AAR-led consortium for procurement of two Airbus A330-200 aircraft. Agreement specified delivery timeline and payment structure. However, immediately after signing, consortium informed NAC that actual seller would be a "Special Purpose Company" not evaluated during bidding—a clear violation of procurement rules.

### Event 6
- **Date**: 2017-02-21
- **Title**: HiFly X Ireland Limited Shell Company Incorporated
- **Description**: HiFly X Ireland Limited was incorporated in Dublin, Ireland, as a special purpose vehicle with paid-up capital of only $1 (or 1 Euro). This classic shell company was created by Portuguese leasing firm HiFly and AAR associates specifically to handle the NAC transaction. The use of an Irish entity raised red flags about tax evasion and money laundering, as Ireland is known as a tax haven. NAC was asked to sign final Sales and Purchase Agreement with this entity that had no assets, eliminating NAC's legal recourse if aircraft were defective or never delivered.

### Event 7
- **Date**: 2017
- **Title**: Contract Transferred to Shell Company; Payments Begin
- **Description**: Despite glaring risks, NAC management led by MD Sugat Ratna Kansakar and endorsed by board proceeded to transfer purchase contract from evaluated consortium to HiFly X Ireland Limited. NAC began transferring funds totaling $209.6 million through escrow agent Norton Rose Fulbright (international law firm) without requisite bank guarantees. Payments flowed from escrow to HiFly X Ireland, from where money trail becomes opaque. US DOJ later confirmed AAR Corp used "intermediaries" to pay $2.5 million in bribes to Nepali officials from these funds.

### Event 8
- **Date**: 2017
- **Title**: Technical Specifications Altered Post-Bidding
- **Description**: NAC management altered critical technical specifications after contract signing. Original RFP specified 242-tonne MTOW aircraft, but NAC accepted 230-tonne MTOW aircraft without any price reduction. This 12-tonne downgrade directly reduced fuel and payload capacity, making long-haul routes to London and Sydney commercially unviable. The 242T variant commands significantly higher market price than 230T variant. CIAA later estimated this specific discrepancy resulted in direct loss of $13.38 million (NPR 1.47 billion). Officials did not merely make mistake—they accepted non-compliant product that permanently handicapped airline's strategic potential.

### Event 9
- **Date**: 2018-06-28
- **Title**: First Aircraft Delivered (9N-ALY "Annapurna")
- **Description**: First Airbus A330-200 aircraft (registration 9N-ALY, named "Annapurna") was delivered to Nepal Airlines. Technical inspections immediately revealed aircraft was certified for only 230 tonnes MTOW instead of contracted 242 tonnes. The discrepancy was known to NAC management but not disclosed publicly. Aircraft entered service despite not meeting original specifications.

### Event 10
- **Date**: 2018-07-26
- **Title**: Second Aircraft Delivered (9N-ALZ "Makalu")
- **Description**: Second Airbus A330-200 aircraft (registration 9N-ALZ, named "Makalu") was delivered to Nepal Airlines. Like the first aircraft, it was certified for only 230 tonnes MTOW. Both aircraft had approximately 1,000 flight hours, confirming they were essentially new despite being procured under "old aircraft" clause. Public and media scrutiny intensified as details of the flawed deal began emerging.

### Event 11
- **Date**: 2018
- **Title**: Office of Auditor General Flags Violations
- **Description**: Office of the Auditor General's 55th Annual Report was first official document to flag procedural violations in the wide-body procurement. Report highlighted irregularities in procurement process, use of intermediaries instead of manufacturer, and suspicious price escalation. This triggered parliamentary interest in investigating the deal.

### Event 12
- **Date**: 2018-12
- **Title**: Public Accounts Committee Launches Investigation
- **Description**: Federal Parliament's Public Accounts Committee (PAC) formed sub-committee led by MP Rajan KC to conduct deep-dive inquiry into wide-body procurement. Sub-committee reviewed thousands of pages of procurement files, interviewed NAC officials, board members, and ministry officials. Investigation examined financial transactions, technical specifications, procurement procedures, and decision-making process.

### Event 13
- **Date**: 2019-01-03
- **Title**: PAC Report Delivered—NPR 4.35 Billion Loss Confirmed
- **Description**: PAC sub-committee delivered explosive 58-page report concluding that wide-body procurement caused loss of NPR 4.35 billion (approximately $37 million) to the state. Report labeled NAC Managing Director Sugat Ratna Kansakar as "kingpin" of scheme. Loss calculation included: MTOW downgrade ($8.4M), artificial price escalation ($6.8M), lost discounts ($23.6M), and operational waste. Report found deal violated Public Procurement Act and NAC bylaws by tailor-making tender for particular supplier, illegally specifying brands, and using "old aircraft" clause as loophole. Report suspected HiFly X Ireland was "fictitious" company designed to obscure money trail and enable tax evasion.

### Event 14
- **Date**: 2019-01-08
- **Title**: PAC Recommends Legal Action Against 32 Individuals
- **Description**: Full PAC committee endorsed sub-committee findings and recommendations. PAC urged government and CIAA to take legal action against dozens of individuals: sitting Tourism Minister Rabindra Adhikari (for releasing final payments), former ministers Jeevan Bahadur Shahi and Jitendra Narayan Dev (for approving initial payments), former Tourism Secretaries Prem Kumar Rai, Shankar Prasad Adhikari, and Krishna Prasad Devkota (who chaired NAC board), and entire NAC leadership team. PAC singled out MD Kansakar for immediate suspension and accountability. Kansakar resigned amid furor.

### Event 15
- **Date**: 2019-02
- **Title**: Tourism Minister Rabindra Adhikari Dies in Helicopter Crash
- **Description**: Tourism Minister Rabindra Adhikari, who had been flagged by PAC for negligence in releasing final payments, died in helicopter crash. His death removed one of the key figures from potential prosecution, though he had been accused of administrative negligence rather than direct corruption.

### Event 16
- **Date**: 2019-2024
- **Title**: CIAA Investigation "Deep Freeze" Period
- **Description**: Despite PAC's scathing report, CIAA investigation stalled for nearly five years. This period of inaction raised serious questions about political interference. The "Deep Freeze" coincided with appointment of Prem Kumar Rai—one of key individuals named in PAC report—as Chief Commissioner of CIAA. This created institutional paradox: head of anti-corruption body was primary suspect in very case his agency was supposed to investigate. Public frustration grew over lack of accountability.

### Event 17
- **Date**: 2024-04-04
- **Title**: CIAA Files Corruption Charges Against 32 Individuals
- **Description**: After years of silence, CIAA filed charge sheet at Special Court in Kathmandu, naming 32 defendants (30 individuals and 2 companies), seeking recovery of NPR 1.47 billion. Accused included: former Tourism Minister Jeevan Bahadur Shahi, former Tourism Secretaries Shankar Prasad Adhikari and Shishir Kumar Dhungana, former NAC MD Sugat Ratna Kansakar, Joint Secretary Buddhi Sagar Lamichhane, multiple NAC board members and staff, and foreign nationals representing AAR Corp, German Aviation Capital, and HiFly. Conspicuously absent from charge sheet was Prem Kumar Rai. CIAA's rationale appeared to be that corruption occurred during execution (2017), not initiation (2016) when Rai was in charge—a distinction later fiercely attacked by Special Court.

### Event 18
- **Date**: 2024-12-05
- **Title**: Special Court Verdict—10 Convicted, 21 Acquitted
- **Description**: Special Court bench comprising Chairman Tek Narayan Kunwar and Members Tej Narayan Singh Rai and Ritendra Thapa delivered verdict. Court convicted 10 individuals and upheld CIAA's claim of NPR 1.47 billion in losses. Convicted: Sugat Ratna Kansakar (2 years 9 months, reduced to 50% due to age over 74), Shankar Prasad Adhikari (1 year 9 months plus NPR 122.59M fine), Shishir Kumar Dhungana (1 year 6 months plus fine), Buddhi Sagar Lamichhane (1 year 6 months plus fine), and several foreign nationals convicted in absentia (Deepak Sharma, Christian Nuehlen, others—1 year 6 months). Court acquitted 21 individuals including former Tourism Minister Jeevan Bahadur Shahi, ruling that while corruption occurred, responsibility lay primarily with top decision-makers who orchestrated and approved flawed procurement. Court concluded prosecution failed to provide direct evidence that Shahi personally benefited or directly ordered technical deviation.

### Event 19
- **Date**: 2024-12 (late)
- **Title**: AAR Corp Settles with US DOJ—$55 Million Fine
- **Description**: In parallel development, US Department of Justice concluded Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) investigation into AAR Corp. AAR admitted to paying $2.5 million in bribes to Nepali officials through intermediaries to secure wide-body aircraft sale. Company agreed to pay approximately $55 million in fines under settlement with DOJ and SEC. Former AAR executive Deepak Sharma pleaded guilty in US court, confessing he conspired to bribe Nepali officials and helped draft NAC's tender requirements to favor AAR's bid. This external confirmation underscored that NAC procurement was transnational bribery scheme, not merely procedural lapse.

### Event 20
- **Date**: 2025-07
- **Title**: Special Court's Full Written Verdict Released—Criticizes CIAA
- **Description**: Special Court's complete written verdict (made public mid-2025) contained unexpected rebuke of CIAA itself. Court criticized anti-graft agency for "selective prosecution," noting some officials deeply involved in decision-making were left off charge-sheet without clear rationale. Court specifically pointed out that Prem Kumar Rai—former Tourism Secretary who convened NAC's board and kick-started wide-body purchase in 2016, and who later became CIAA Chief Commissioner—was not charged despite his early role. Verdict stated: "The procurement process began on April 14, 2016... chaired by then Tourism Secretary Prem Kumar Rai. The CIAA did not challenge the legality of that board decision... Adopting selective approach—prosecuting some while excluding others involved in same act—demonstrates biased and discriminatory attitude." Court essentially accused CIAA of protecting its own Chief Commissioner, undermining credibility of entire prosecution.

### Event 21
- **Date**: 2025-12-02
- **Title**: CIAA Appeals to Supreme Court
- **Description**: CIAA, dissatisfied with acquittals and stung by Special Court's criticism, filed appeal at Supreme Court seeking to convict remaining accused who were acquitted. Appeal challenges Special Court's decision on two primary grounds: (1) Vicarious Liability of Minister—arguing Minister cannot be absolved of responsibility for multi-billion rupee procurement under his ministry, and that approval of price escalation and acceptance of flawed aircraft required ministerial consent, making Shahi complicit; (2) Inadequacy of Punishment—arguing acquittals of 14 NAC employees and board members were erroneous, as their silence and participation in sub-committees facilitated crime. Case remains pending at Supreme Court as of December 2025.
